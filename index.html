Segmentation Challenge

Overview

The Segmentation Challenge Lübeck (SCL) is an international challenge, which addresses any researcher or workgroup in the field of computational science. The aim is the segmentation of brain lesions in multimodal MRI image data after an ischemic stroke. The lowest possible error of the segmented brain areas is intended.

Therefore we are providing the download of anonymous datasets of real patients. After you have trained and tested your algorithms you can submit your segmented image data. An automatic evaluation will rank you or your team automatically after uploading your data online.

This challenge will be reviewed as a workshop in October 5th or 9th in context of the MICCAI 2015 in Munich, handy to the German Oktoberfest from October 4th to 19th. The best three participants of this challenge will be compelled to present their used algorithms. After the workshop there will be the possibility for a journal publication.

About

Motivation

A stroke or colloquially brain attack is a sudden occurrence of brain disease, which often involves the loss of functions of the central nervous system and is caused by disturbance in the blood supply of the brain. Strokes can be classified into the groups ischemic, caused by undersupply of blood in a brain area, and hemorrhagic, caused by undersupply and pressure of leaking blood in a brain area due to a broken vessel.

Fig. 1: Defect of the blood-brain barrier after stroke shown in MRI. T1-weighted images, left image without right image with contrast medium administration.

These lesions can be identified (segmented) in multimodal MRI imaging data. With this challenge, we aim to take a step forward to computer-aided methods for automatic segmentation of brain lesions. One clinical application could be the better understanding of specific brain area functions by connecting the caused dysfunction with the affected brain area. The required processes would be strongly simplified by automatic segmentation algorithms.

Evaluation

In the following our used distance measures are presented. They are used to describe the degree of similarity of the segmentations and the reference. The references are manually segmentations by experts.

Dice's Coefficient

Like the Jacquard similarity index, the Dice's coefficient measures the similarity of two data sets or images. The formula for two sets of points \(A\) and \(B\) is given as

$$Dice=\dfrac{2|A\cap B|}{|A|+|B|}.$$

It enunciates, that the overlap \(A\cap B\) of both sets is weighted with \(2\) and then divided by the number of points of both sets. A value of 0 indicates no overlap, a value of 1 indicates perfect similarity.

Fig. 2: The two sets \(A\), \(B\) and their union are shown.

Precision & Recall

The \(precision\) (also called positive predictive value) and \(recall\) (also known as sensitivity) of two sets can be defined as

$$precision=\dfrac{TP}{TP+FP}$$

and

$$recall=\dfrac{TP}{TP+FN},$$

where \(TP\) (true positive) are the overlapping points, \(FP\) (false positive) are the wrong segmented points in the segmentation and \(FN\) (false negative) are the not segmented points in the reference, exemplified in figure 3.

Fig. 3: Green circle: segmented set points. Gray circle: reference points.

Hausdorff distance & ASSD

For two given non-empty subsets \(A\) and \(B\) of a metric space \((M, d)\), where \(d\) is the euclidean distance, the Hausdorff distance \(H\) can be defined as

$$H(A,B)=\text{max}\{\max_{a\in A}\min_{b\in B}d(a,b),\max_{b\in B}\min_{a\in A}d(a,b)\}.$$

The Hausdorff distance is the greatest distance of all the distances from a point in one set to the closest point in the other set. The directed Hausdorff distance can be defined as

$$h(A,B)=\underset{a\in A}{\text{max }}\underset{b\in B}{\text{min }}d(a,b),$$

which is visualized in Figure 4.

The average symmetric surface distance \(ASSD\) is calculated by

$$ASSD=\dfrac{\sum\limits_{a \in A}\min\limits_{b \in B}d(a,b)+\sum\limits_{b \in B}\min\limits_{a \in A}d(a,b)}{|A|+|B|}.$$

For every border point in a set of points, the distance to the closest border point in the other set is calculated and divided by the number of all considered points.

Fig. 4: The directed Hausdorff distance \(h\) is shown in an example from \(A\) to \(B\) and from \(B\) to \(A\).

Ranking

  1. For each patient dataset the five distance measures are calculated.
  2. Subsequently each of the five distance measures are ranked, which leads to five ranks per patient dataset.
  3. Finally the average rank for all patient datasets is calculated and the overall rank determined.
